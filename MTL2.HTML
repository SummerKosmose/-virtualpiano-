Chords


Chords happen when three or more notes are played at the same time. When only two notes are played together, it creates a harmony. Chords, however, contain three or more notes and are responsible for setting the emotion emotional tone of music.

In a song, chords are almost always in the same key. If you play a bunch of notes in the key of C major, you're playing some kind of C major chord.

The most common chords are called triads. They contain three notes from the major scale: the root (or first note), : the third note, : the fifth note.

For example, in C major, start with C, skip D and play E, then skip F and play G. The notes C, E, and G form a C major chord.

If you move this same shape along all the white keys, you'll get different chords within the key of C major.

In music, there are four types of triads, but the two most common are major and minor triads. Major chords sound brighter and happier, while minor chords sound darker and a little sadder.

To understand chords better, let's analyze them by counting semitones. From C to E, there are four semitones. From E to G, there are three semitones. Therefore, the formula for a major chord is four semitones followed by three semitones. So it's C - E - G

What happens if you flip that formula? Just like flipping emotions, it changes from happy to sad. Four and three become three and four. Starting from C, count three semitones to reach E-flat, and then count four more semitones to reach G. This creates a C minor triad.

Let's review the major scale formula, which is: whole step, whole step, half step, whole step, whole step, whole step, and half step.[W W H W W W H]

We can build a chord by selecting every other note in the scale. Moving this pattern up the scale creates additional chords. By analyzing each chord, we can determine whether it is major or minor.

The first chord, G major, follows the pattern of four semitones and then three semitones, so it is a major chord. Moving up one scale degree gives us A minor, which follows the pattern of three semitones and then four semitones. This pattern remains the same in every major key.

The final chord sounds a little different. It is neither major nor minor. Instead, it is called a diminished chord. Counting the semitones shows that there are three semitones between B and D, and another three between D and F, giving it the formula of three plus three. In C major, the chords are: C major, D minor, E minor, F major, G major, A minor, and B diminished.

To apply this system to any key, musicians label chords with Roman numerals. Uppercase numerals represent major chords, while lowercase numerals represent minor chords. In C major, C major becomes I, D minor becomes ii, E minor becomes iii, F major becomes IV, G major becomes V, A minor becomes vi, and B diminished becomes vii°.

Using Roman numerals allows us to create chord progressions in any key. One of the most popular progressions in pop music is I–V–vi–IV. In the key of C major, this progression uses the chords C major, G major, A minor, and F major.

Let's try the same progression in F major. Following the major-key chord pattern of major, minor, minor, major, major, minor, and diminished, we get F major as the I chord, C major as the V chord, D minor as the vi chord, and B-flat major as the IV chord.
